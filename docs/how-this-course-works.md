This page covers the practical side: how the course is structured, what a week looks like, how a lecture runs, how to keep up when life gets busy, and what you can expect from us in return. Read it before you enroll so there are no surprises.

## The weekly cadence

Each week is four live lectures plus the work you do between them. The live time is roughly five to six hours. The work between sessions is where the learning actually happens, so plan on **8 to 12 hours a week total** if you want the outcome.

A typical week runs like this:

- **Lecture days.** You show up, the instructor teaches a concept and then immediately solves problems with it on camera. You are expected to attend live when you can, because asking questions in the moment is most of the value of a cohort.
- **Between lectures.** You solve the assigned problems from the problem bank. This is not optional padding. The people who get the result are the ones who do the problems, not just the ones who watch.
- **End of week.** You should be able to take a fresh problem in that week's topic and recognize the pattern, pick the structure, write the C++, and state the complexity. If you can't, that is your signal to rewatch and redo before the next week stacks on top.

The course is cumulative. Week 7 assumes weeks 1 through 6 are in your hands. That is deliberate, and it is why falling a week behind costs more than it looks like it should. The schedule in the [repo](../README.md) tells you exactly what to watch and what to solve, in order, every week.

## How a lecture is structured

Every lecture follows the same shape, on purpose, so the rhythm becomes familiar and you spend your attention on the material instead of the format.

1. **Concept.** The instructor explains the idea and why it exists. What problem does this structure or algorithm solve, what does it cost, and when would you actually reach for it. Short, concrete, no hand-waving.

2. **Build it by hand in C++.** Before you are allowed to use the STL version, you watch the thing get built from scratch and you build it yourself. Your own dynamic array before `std::vector`. Your own hash table before `unordered_map`. Your own heap before `priority_queue`. You see the false starts and the cleanup, not a finished file that appears out of nowhere. Then, once you understand what the tool does and what it costs, we switch to the STL and treat it as the professional default.

3. **Drill problems.** The rest of the lecture is problems. Taught, then drilled. You watch the thinking, the dead ends, and the fixes, because that is what solving a problem actually looks like and it is the part most courses hide. Then you do more of them on your own between sessions.

Every problem, on camera, runs through the same five-step method we use all 16 weeks until it is automatic. We call it **UPSCR**: Understand the problem and work a small example by hand, identify the Pattern, choose a Strategy and state its complexity before typing, write the Code, then Review by tracing the code and checking edge cases. The order is the lesson. Beginners jump straight to code and get stuck. The framework forces the thinking up front, where it is cheap.

## How to keep up

Being honest: this is 64 lectures and more than four hundred problems over four months. People fall behind. Here is how to not let a slow week turn into a dropped course.

- **Protect the problem time, not just the lecture time.** If you only have a few hours in a given week, spend them on problems, not on rewatching at 1x. Watching feels productive and mostly isn't. Solving is the reps.
- **Use the schedule as a checklist.** The repo lays out, week by week, exactly what to watch and what to solve. You never have to wonder what's next. Follow the order.
- **Don't let two weeks stack.** Because the material is cumulative, one missed week is recoverable and two missed weeks is a hole. If you slip, catch up on the recordings and the problems before the next topic lands on top of the gap.
- **Redo problems you got stuck on.** The goal is recognition on sight. If a problem fought you, come back to it a few days later and solve it cold. That is how a pattern moves from "I've seen it" to "I can do it."

If you miss a lecture entirely, it is not a missed topic. You watch the recording and catch up on that lecture's problems. Nothing is lost, the sequence just waits for you in the repo.

## Support model

This is a taught course with other people in it, not a pile of videos you watch alone and abandon.

- **Live questions.** The main support channel is the lecture itself. You are in the room with the instructor and around 30 other people solving the same problems at the same time. Ask when you're confused. That real-time back-and-forth is the reason a cohort beats a recording.
- **A cohort, not a crowd.** The group is kept small, around 30, so the pace and the problem-solving stay coherent and questions actually get answered. Seats are limited for that reason.
- **The repo as your map.** Curriculum, the week-by-week schedule, the full problem bank, a pattern reference you can grep, and the projects all live in the public repo. It is the study companion that keeps everyone pointed at the same next step.
