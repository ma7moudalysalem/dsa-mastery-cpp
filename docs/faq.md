# FAQ

Honest answers to the questions people actually ask before enrolling in **Data Structures & Algorithms in C++ — From Fundamentals to Interview-Ready**. If something here makes you decide this isn't the right course for you, that's a good outcome. The wrong fit helps no one.

---

### Do I need to know C++ already?

You need the basics: variables and types, `if`/`else`, loops, functions, and arrays. You should be able to write a small program that reads input, does something in a loop, and prints a result, and compile it yourself.

You do **not** need pointers, dynamic memory, templates, the STL, classes, or any data structures going in. Those are the course. We teach what you need when you need it.

### Why C++ and not Python?

Because C++ makes you see what the machine is doing. Memory, copies, and cost are visible, so the complexity lessons land as something concrete instead of a formula you memorize. When you write your own dynamic array and watch it reallocate, "amortized O(1)" stops being a phrase and becomes a thing you can point at.

C++ is also widely accepted in technical interviews. Where manual memory management teaches a concept, we use it. Where the STL is the professional default, we use the STL. You finish knowing both the tool and what it costs.

If you refuse to write C++, this is the wrong course, and that's fine. Pick one that matches the language you want.

### Is this course for beginners?

It depends on what you mean. If you've never programmed before, no. Learn basic programming first, in any language, then come back. We will not spend lectures explaining what an `if` statement is, and you'd be lost from week one.

If you can already write small programs in some language and you're new to data structures and algorithms specifically, yes. That's exactly the person this is built for. You bring basic coding ability; we build the DSA from the ground up, starting with the analysis tools before any structure.

### Do I need a computer science degree?

No. The course assumes no formal CS background. We start with complexity analysis and recursion from first principles in weeks 1 and 2, precisely so that everyone is working from the same foundation regardless of how they got here. Plenty of strong engineers are self-taught. What matters is whether you'll do the work, not what's on your transcript.

### How much time per week should I budget?

Four lectures a week, so roughly 5 to 6 hours of live time, plus problem solving between sessions. Plan on **8 to 12 hours a week total** for the full 16 weeks.

That range is real, not a sales-friendly lowball. The live lectures are the smaller part. The result comes from the problems you solve between them. People who only watch the lectures spend their time and don't get the outcome. People who do the problem bank do.

### Are the lectures live or recorded?

Live, on a schedule, in a small cohort of around 30 people. Four sessions a week, each 75 to 90 minutes, for 16 weeks, so 64 lectures.

Every session is recorded, and you keep lifetime access to the recordings and all materials. A missed lecture is never a missed topic. The live format exists because solving problems alongside other people on the same schedule keeps you moving in a way that a pile of pre-recorded videos rarely does.

### What if I fall behind or miss a week?

You watch the recordings and catch up on the problems. The week-by-week schedule in the repo tells you exactly what to do and in what order, so catching up is a matter of following the list, not guessing what you missed.

Life happens. The course is built to survive a bad week. What it can't survive is you quietly stopping the problems for a month, because the value is cumulative and each phase builds on the last. If you fall behind, prioritize getting back to solving problems over watching every minute of every recording.

### Will this actually get me interview-ready?

It covers the data structures, algorithms, and patterns that technical interviews at strong companies draw from, and you'll have solved more than four hundred problems by the end, most of them worked on camera so you see the thinking and not just a finished answer. That's the material and the reps.

Here's the honest boundary. The course gives you the preparation. Passing an interview also depends on how much you practice on your own and how well you communicate under pressure. We drill both, including stating complexity out loud the way you would in a real interview. We can't sit in the room for you. Someone who does the work here walks in able to recognize the pattern, pick the right approach, write correct C++, and reason through a medium-hard problem. That's what "interview-ready" means and that's what we deliver.

### How is this different from the free resources online?

Free material is everywhere, and a lot of it is good. The problem is that it's scattered, inconsistent in depth, and gives you no sequence, no schedule, and nobody solving problems with you. Assembling a coherent 16-week path out of it is most of the work, and that work is what you're paying for here.

Concretely, this course gives you an ordered curriculum where complexity and recursion come first as the lens for everything after, a pattern-first approach built around the 28 interview patterns drilled until you recognize them on sight, every structure built by hand before you use the STL version, and a live cohort on a fixed schedule. The graph and DP coverage is complete rather than a sampler, including Dijkstra, Bellman-Ford, Floyd-Warshall, both MST algorithms, and DP on grids and trees. If you have the discipline to build all of that yourself from free sources, you genuinely may not need a paid course. Most people don't, and that gap is the point.

### Do you cover the STL, or do we build everything by hand?

Both, in that order. You implement each structure by hand first, your own dynamic array before `std::vector`, your own hash table before `unordered_map`, your own heap before `priority_queue`, so you understand what it does and what it costs. Then we switch to the STL and treat it as the professional default, which is what you'd reach for in real work and in most interviews.
